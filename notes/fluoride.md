# Fluoride and human health: a century of science reviewed

**Fluoride's effects on the human body range from well-established skeletal and dental damage at high doses to increasingly credible evidence of neurotoxicity in children, with the scientific consensus now shifting faster than at any point in the compound's 80-year history as a public health intervention.** The National Toxicology Program concluded in 2024 with moderate confidence that fluoride exposure above 1.5 mg/L in drinking water is consistently associated with lower IQ in children. A federal judge ruled that same year that fluoridation at even the U.S. recommended level of 0.7 mg/L poses an "unreasonable risk" of reducing children's IQ. Meanwhile, major dental and medical organizations continue to endorse community water fluoridation, and the case remains under appeal. What follows is a comprehensive review of all major findings from approximately 1920 to early 2026, ordered from the most severe health impacts supported by the strongest evidence to findings with weaker or more speculative support.

---

## 1. Skeletal fluorosis: crippling bone disease at high chronic exposure

**Evidence strength: Strong | Severity: High**

Skeletal fluorosis is the most severe and best-documented health consequence of chronic fluoride overexposure. It progresses through three clinical stages: Stage I involves joint stiffness and pain with subtle bone changes; Stage II brings chronic arthritis, ligament calcification, and reduced mobility; Stage III ("crippling" fluorosis) causes severe skeletal deformity, neurological complications, and near-total immobility.

The condition results from sustained intake of **10–25 mg/day over many years**, though a Chinese study documented Stage I clinical fluorosis at daily doses as low as **6.5 mg/day**. The NRC's 2006 landmark review confirmed that the EPA's maximum contaminant level goal of 4 mg/L was not protective against skeletal fluorosis. A 2023 dose-response meta-analysis by Veneri et al. in *Current Osteoporosis Reports* found a summary relative risk of **2.05 (95% CI: 1.60–2.64)** for skeletal fluorosis at highest versus lowest fluoride exposure categories, with an almost linear positive relationship from low concentrations up to 5 mg/L.

Skeletal fluorosis is endemic in **over 25 countries**, predominantly India and China, where an estimated 100 million people are affected. In contrast, only five cases have ever been documented in the United States, all at intakes of 15–20 mg/day sustained for decades. At the U.S. recommended fluoridation level of 0.7 mg/L, skeletal fluorosis is not a realistic concern for the general population. However, individuals with chronic kidney disease accumulate up to **four times more fluoride** in bones and represent a genuinely vulnerable subgroup.

## 2. Neurotoxicity in children: the IQ evidence that changed the debate

**Evidence strength: Moderate-to-strong | Severity: High**

The most consequential development in fluoride science over the past two decades is the accumulating evidence linking fluoride exposure to reduced cognitive development in children, particularly IQ decrements. This body of research has fundamentally shifted the terms of the fluoridation debate.

**The foundational meta-analysis** by Choi et al. (2012), published in *Environmental Health Perspectives* by researchers at Harvard and China Medical University, analyzed 27 epidemiological studies involving more than 8,000 children. The pooled standardized mean difference in IQ was **−0.45 (95% CI: −0.56, −0.35)**, equivalent to roughly **7 IQ points** on a standard scale. Twenty-six of 27 studies found an inverse association. However, the study drew criticism for relying primarily on Chinese cross-sectional studies with ecological exposure measures, inadequate control of confounders like arsenic co-exposure and socioeconomic status, and exposure levels well above U.S. fluoridation concentrations. Harvard itself appended a clarification noting the results did not allow judgments about risk at U.S. levels.

**The ELEMENT cohort study** (Bashash et al., 2017, *Environmental Health Perspectives*) marked a turning point as the first major prospective study using individual-level prenatal fluoride biomarkers. Among 299 mother-child pairs in Mexico City, a 0.5 mg/L increase in maternal urinary fluoride predicted a **3.15-point decrease** in General Cognitive Index at age 4 and a **2.50-point IQ decrease** at ages 6–12, after adjusting for maternal IQ, education, and multiple other confounders. Crucially, the effect was driven by prenatal, not postnatal, exposure. A 2022 follow-up with expanded sample sizes (N≈348–386) confirmed approximately a **2-point IQ decrement per 0.5 mg/L increase** and found greater deficits in non-verbal/Performance IQ than verbal abilities.

**The MIREC study** (Green et al., 2019, *JAMA Pediatrics*) extended these findings to Canada. Among 512 mother-child pairs from six Canadian cities, each 1 mg/L increase in maternal urinary fluoride was associated with a **4.49-point lower Full-Scale IQ in boys** (95% CI: −8.38, −0.60), though the association was not statistically significant in girls. Each additional milligram of estimated daily fluoride intake correlated with a **3.66-point IQ decrease** across both sexes. This study was significant because it involved exposure levels relevant to community water fluoridation at 0.7 mg/L. Critics argued about measurement limitations and published detailed methodological objections.

**The NTP systematic review** (final monograph, August 2024) represents the most comprehensive assessment to date, evaluating 72+ epidemiological studies from 12 countries. The NTP concluded with **"moderate confidence"** that fluoride exposure above 1.5 mg/L is consistently associated with lower IQ in children, finding that 18 of 19 high-quality studies showed an inverse fluoride-IQ association. The companion meta-analysis published in *JAMA Pediatrics* (Taylor et al., January 2025) found that every 1 mg/L increase in urinary fluoride corresponded to a **1.63-point decrease in children's IQ**. Among high-quality studies, inverse associations persisted even below 1.5 mg/L when measured in urine. The NTP explicitly stated its review was not designed to evaluate community water fluoridation at 0.7 mg/L and found insufficient data to draw conclusions at that level.

**Counterevidence is important to note.** The Dunedin study (Broadbent et al., 2015, *American Journal of Public Health*) — a well-designed New Zealand longitudinal cohort following ~1,000 individuals from birth to age 38 — found no clear IQ differences from fluoride exposure. A large Swedish retrospective cohort (Aggeborn & Öhman, 2021) estimated zero cognitive effect. Australian longitudinal studies by Do et al. (2023, 2025) found no association between early childhood fluoridated water exposure and cognitive outcomes or executive function. A Spanish cohort (Ibarluzea et al., 2022) found a positive correlation between maternal fluoride and some cognitive measures in boys. These null findings predominantly come from well-designed studies in countries with regulated water fluoridation, raising the possibility that effects are confined to higher exposures or that study designs measuring water-level exposure (rather than individual biomarkers) lack sensitivity.

**Mechanistic support** from animal and in vitro research is substantial. Fluoride crosses the blood-brain barrier and placenta. Documented mechanisms include oxidative stress and depletion of antioxidant enzymes, neuronal apoptosis via caspase pathway activation, mitochondrial dysfunction, disruption of cholinergic, glutamatergic, dopaminergic, and serotonergic neurotransmission, synaptic dysfunction, and neuroinflammation through NF-κB pathway activation and microglial stimulation. Histopathological changes have been documented in the hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, and cerebellum of exposed animals.

## 3. Dental fluorosis has risen sharply alongside expanding fluoride sources

**Evidence strength: Strong | Severity: Moderate (cosmetic at mild levels; adverse at severe)**

Dental fluorosis — white spots, streaks, or brown staining of tooth enamel caused by fluoride exposure during tooth development — is the most visible marker of overexposure. CDC data from NHANES show a dramatic increase: prevalence among U.S. adolescents aged 12–15 rose from **22.6% in 1986–1987 to 40.6% in 1999–2004**, with moderate-to-severe cases increasing from 1.3% to 3.6%. Later NHANES data (2011–2016) showed approximately **61% of adolescents affected**, though CDC cautioned that some increase may reflect examiner variability rather than true prevalence changes.

This rising trend was a key reason the U.S. Public Health Service lowered its recommended fluoridation level in 2015 from a range of **0.7–1.2 mg/L to a single level of 0.7 mg/L** — the first change in over 50 years. The reduction was expected to decrease fluoride intake from drinking water by approximately 25%. Nevertheless, the 2015 Cochrane Review estimated that at 0.7 mg/L, roughly **12% of the exposed population** would develop dental fluorosis of aesthetic concern.

The NRC 2006 committee unanimously concluded that severe dental fluorosis constitutes an **adverse health effect** — not merely a cosmetic issue — at 4 mg/L, noting structural tooth damage and loss of function. Mild and very mild fluorosis, affecting the vast majority of cases, is generally considered cosmetic. The severity is dose-dependent and occurs only during enamel formation (primarily before age 8), making children the exclusively vulnerable population.

## 4. Thyroid disruption and fluoride as an endocrine disruptor

**Evidence strength: Moderate | Severity: Moderate**

The NRC's 2006 report delivered perhaps its most underappreciated finding when it concluded that **fluoride is an endocrine disruptor** capable of altering normal endocrine function, particularly thyroid activity. This conclusion has gained increasing attention as thyroid dysfunction prevalence has risen globally.

Historically, fluoride was prescribed as a **thyroid-suppressing medication** in Europe and South America through the 1950s for hyperthyroidism, at doses of 2–5 mg/day — overlapping with total daily fluoride intake in some fluoridated communities. The NRC found that "several lines of information indicate an effect of fluoride exposure on thyroid function," including elevated TSH, increased goiter prevalence, and altered T3/T4 levels, particularly when iodine intake is deficient.

The most cited epidemiological study is **Peckham et al. (2015)**, published in the *Journal of Epidemiology and Community Health*, which analyzed hypothyroidism rates across 7,935 GP practices in England. Areas with fluoride above 0.7 mg/L had **62% higher odds** of high hypothyroidism prevalence (OR 1.62, 95% CI: 1.38–1.90) compared to areas below 0.3 mg/L. Critics noted this was an ecological study susceptible to the ecological fallacy, failed to control for iodine intake, and was rated "low quality" by Ireland's Department of Health. A 2024 dose-response meta-analysis by Veneri et al. across 28 studies found a non-linear effect on thyroid function, with increasing TSH in children above a threshold of exposure and a positive association with goiter and hypothyroidism risk — primarily at high exposures above 2 mg/L. The EU Scientific Committee on Health and Environmental Risks concluded that systematic evaluation "does not suggest a potential thyroid effect at realistic exposures."

Other endocrine effects documented in the NRC report include altered parathyroid hormone secretion (secondary hyperparathyroidism in skeletal fluorosis), potential effects on insulin sensitivity and glucose metabolism, and reproductive hormone changes in men at exposures of 3–27 mg/day (elevated FSH, reduced testosterone and inhibin-B). NHANES-based analyses found no significant associations at U.S.-relevant exposure levels.

## 5. Fluoride accumulates in the pineal gland more than any other soft tissue

**Evidence strength: Strong for accumulation; Moderate for functional effects**

Jennifer Luke's groundbreaking doctoral research at the University of Surrey (1997), published in *Caries Research* (2001), established that the human pineal gland contains the **highest fluoride concentration of any soft tissue in the body**. Analyzing 11 aged human pineal glands, she found a mean fluoride concentration of **297 mg/kg wet weight** — compared to just 0.5 mg/kg in corresponding muscle tissue. Individual calcified pineal specimens contained up to **21,000 ppm fluoride**, exceeding levels found in bones of skeletal fluorosis patients. The concentration correlated strongly with pineal calcium content (r = 0.73) because the pineal gland uniquely calcifies via hydroxyapatite crystals — the same mineral matrix in teeth and bones that avidly binds fluoride.

The functional consequences are biologically plausible but less well established. In Luke's controlled animal study on Mongolian gerbils, high-fluoride animals produced **significantly less melatonin** during prepubertal development, and female high-fluoride gerbils showed **significantly accelerated onset of puberty**. The NRC 2006 report acknowledged that "fluoride is likely to cause decreased melatonin production and to have other effects on normal pineal function" and called for additional human research — research that remains largely unperformed two decades later.

Two population-based studies (Malin et al., 2019, *Environmental Health*; Malin et al., 2021) using NHANES and Canadian Health Measures Survey data found associations between higher fluoride exposure and increased sleep disturbances, daytime sleepiness, and shorter sleep duration in adolescents. These findings are consistent with melatonin disruption but cannot establish causation. Independent research has confirmed that pineal calcification (regardless of cause) correlates with decreased REM sleep, reduced total sleep time, and lower sleep efficiency.

A notable historical finding: in the Newburgh-Kingston fluoridation trial (1945–1955), girls in fluoridated Newburgh reached menarche on average **five months earlier** than girls in unfluoridated Kingston — a finding consistent with pineal/melatonin disruption but not considered significant at the time.

## 6. Bone fracture risk follows a non-linear dose-response pattern

**Evidence strength: Moderate | Severity: Moderate**

Fluoride's relationship with bone is paradoxical: it increases bone mineral density while potentially reducing bone quality. Fluoride ions substitute for hydroxyl ions in hydroxyapatite, creating fluorapatite that is denser but may be more brittle. This was dramatically demonstrated when high-dose fluoride therapy for osteoporosis (popular in the 1990s) was abandoned after clinical trials showed it **increased spinal bone density but also increased non-vertebral fracture rates**.

A comprehensive 2025 dose-response meta-analysis by Veneri et al. in *Environmental Health*, encompassing 37 studies from 1945 to 2024, found a non-linear positive relationship between drinking water fluoride and fracture risk, with a threshold around **1.5 mg/L**. At 2.0 mg/L, the relative risk was 1.06; at 4.0 mg/L, it reached 1.35. A particularly concerning subgroup finding showed that **women over 50 faced elevated fracture risk beginning at approximately 0.5 mg/L** (RR 1.26 at 1.0 mg/L). The Swedish Mammography Cohort Study (Helte et al., 2021, *Environmental Health Perspectives*) found women in the highest third of fluoride consumption had a **59% increased risk of hip fractures**. However, a Swedish nationwide cohort (Näsman et al., 2013) found no association, and a 2015 meta-analysis of 14 observational studies found no significant overall hip fracture risk increase. The NRC 2006 report noted "several strong observational studies" indicating increased fracture risk at 4 mg/L but could not draw definitive conclusions at 2 mg/L.

## 7. Kidney vulnerability creates a high-risk subpopulation

**Evidence strength: Moderate | Severity: Moderate for vulnerable populations**

The kidney is the primary organ for fluoride excretion and, aside from the pineal gland, is exposed to higher fluoride concentrations than any other soft tissue. This creates a concerning feedback loop for individuals with impaired renal function: reduced kidney function leads to decreased fluoride clearance, which elevates serum fluoride, which can cause further kidney damage.

A comprehensive 2019 review by Dharmaratne in *Human and Experimental Toxicology*, spanning approximately 100 years of research, concluded there are "direct adverse effects on the kidneys by excess fluoride, leading to kidney damage and dysfunction," primarily affecting the tubular area. A Chinese study of 210 children found renal damage at drinking water fluoride concentrations of just **2.0 mg/L**. NHANES data (Malin et al., 2022) showed adolescents with lower estimated glomerular filtration rates had significantly higher plasma fluoride per unit of water fluoride. The NRC 2006 report specifically identified individuals with renal impairments as a **"potentially susceptible subpopulation"** who retain more fluoride than healthy people. The National Kidney Foundation has acknowledged this concern but has not issued a formal position opposing fluoridation.

## 8. ADHD and inattention show an emerging but inconsistent link

**Evidence strength: Weak-to-moderate | Severity: Moderate**

Evidence connecting fluoride to attention deficit symptoms has emerged from several research streams but remains inconsistent across exposure metrics. The strongest individual study is Bashash et al. (2018, *Environment International*), which found in the ELEMENT prospective cohort that a 0.5 mg/L increase in maternal urinary fluoride predicted significantly higher DSM-IV Inattention scores (**+2.84 points**, 95% CI: 0.84, 4.84) and ADHD Index scores in children. Notably, associations were specific to **inattention, not hyperactivity**, suggesting fluoride may selectively affect attentional networks rather than behavioral regulation.

Riddell et al. (2019) found that 1 mg/L higher tap water fluoride was associated with **6.1 times higher odds of ADHD diagnosis** in Canadian youth — though urinary fluoride did not significantly predict ADHD, creating an internal inconsistency. The ecological study by Malin and Till (2015) found state-level fluoridation prevalence predicted ADHD prevalence, but Perrott (2018) showed this association disappeared entirely when state altitude was included as a covariate. A 2023 systematic review in *Medicina* noted that heterogeneity of study designs precluded firm conclusions.

## 9. Psychological and behavioral effects remain largely unexplored in humans

**Evidence strength: Weak (animal studies moderate; human evidence sparse) | Severity: Low-to-moderate**

Research on fluoride's psychological effects constitutes one of the largest gaps in the literature. Spittle's 1994 review in *International Clinical Psychopharmacology* synthesized 12 case reports of occupational and therapeutic fluoride exposure, identifying a consistent pattern of **lethargy, weakness, impaired concentration, and memory problems**. However, these occurred at exposure levels far exceeding community water fluoridation.

Animal studies provide more systematic evidence. A 2025 meta-analysis in *Biological Trace Element Research* found prolonged fluoride exposure produced substantial behavioral alterations, with a significant **pro-depressant effect** (β = 1.53, p < 0.001). Li et al. (2019) documented anxiety- and depression-like behaviors in mice exposed to fluoride for 120+ days, linked to decreased BDNF expression and GABA system disruption. Du et al. (2021) showed similar results in juvenile rats, with the antidepressant venlafaxine partially reversing the effects. Intriguingly, Lu et al. (2019) found fluoride **increased serotonin and reduced anxiety behaviors** in mice — suggesting neurochemical disruption that could manifest as either increased or decreased anxiety depending on context.

The only human study directly linking fluoride exposure to internalizing symptoms (Adkins et al., 2022, *Environmental Research*) found higher childhood urinary fluoride associated with increased **somatization** but not depression or anxiety specifically. Chinese studies have found higher depression, anxiety, and lower self-esteem among college students with dental fluorosis, but separating the direct neurological effects of fluoride from the psychosocial impact of disfigured teeth is methodologically challenging.

Claims that fluoride induces "docility" or "submissiveness" in populations trace to a 1987 self-published conspiracy booklet, not to any scientific source. No peer-reviewed study has ever demonstrated such effects. These claims have been dismissed by Holocaust historians and lack documentary support. Mullenix et al. (1995) — the landmark rat behavioral study — actually found **prenatal fluoride caused hyperactivity in males**, contradicting the docility narrative.

## 10. Effects on creativity, abstract thinking, and higher-order cognition

**Evidence strength: No direct evidence; theoretical basis only | Severity: Unknown**

**No published peer-reviewed study has directly examined fluoride's effects on creative thinking, divergent thinking, imaginative capacity, systems thinking, or holistic cognitive processing.** This represents a significant and conspicuous gap in the literature.

Indirect evidence comes from several directions. Many Chinese fluoride-IQ studies employed **Raven's Progressive Matrices**, which specifically tests abstract reasoning, pattern recognition, and fluid intelligence — and consistently found lower scores in high-fluoride populations. Goodman et al. (2022) found prenatal fluoride exposure was more strongly associated with **non-verbal/Performance IQ deficits** (visuospatial reasoning, pattern recognition) than verbal deficits. Since creativity depends heavily on prefrontal cortex function, working memory, and cognitive flexibility — all theoretically vulnerable to neurotoxic disruption — the possibility of effects on higher-order cognition is biologically plausible but entirely undemonstrated. An Ethiopian pilot study found fluoride exposure negatively associated with children's drawing performance, and drawing tasks correlate with executive function, but this is a distant proxy for creativity.

Similarly, no studies have examined fluoride's effects on willpower, motivation, or self-regulation using validated psychological instruments. The closest relevant finding is Spittle's documentation of lethargy in fluoride-exposed workers, which is a toxic effect at high doses rather than evidence of subtle motivational impairment at lower exposures.

## 11. Dose-response thresholds differ sharply across health endpoints

Understanding fluoride's health effects requires grasping the regulatory landscape and how exposure levels map to different outcomes. Current standards reflect decades-old risk assessments that many scientists now consider outdated.

| Regulatory body | Standard | Level | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| US PHS/CDC | Recommended optimal | **0.7 mg/L** | Dental benefit with minimal fluorosis (2015) |
| WHO | Guideline maximum | **1.5 mg/L** | Protection against dental/skeletal fluorosis |
| US EPA | Secondary MCL | **2.0 mg/L** | Moderate dental fluorosis prevention |
| US EPA | Primary MCL | **4.0 mg/L** | Crippling skeletal fluorosis prevention |
| IOM | Upper tolerable intake | **10 mg/day** | Preclinical skeletal fluorosis |

The NRC unanimously concluded in 2006 that the **4 mg/L MCL is not protective** and recommended EPA lower it — a recommendation EPA has never acted on. Total daily fluoride intake for adults in fluoridated communities ranges from **1–3 mg/day** from all sources (water, food, beverages, toothpaste), with drinking water accounting for 40–70% of total intake. Young children who swallow fluoride toothpaste can receive substantially more.

A 2025 rapid systematic review in *Advances in Nutrition* examining 58 studies found **no convincing evidence of harm at 0.7–1.0 mg/L** for any health outcome but reported evidence of adverse neurological and thyroid effects in children above 1.5 mg/L. Grandjean (2019) calculated a threshold drinking water concentration for neurotoxicity at approximately **0.2 mg/L** using benchmark dose analysis — well below current recommended levels — though this estimate is contested. The critical unresolved question is whether the margin of safety between the recommended 0.7 mg/L and the level where harm becomes detectable is adequate, especially considering total exposure from all sources.

## 12. Eighty years of fluoridation history have led to an inflection point

Water fluoridation began when dentist Frederick McKay noticed in 1901 that Colorado Springs residents had stained but cavity-resistant teeth. After ALCOA chemist H.V. Churchill identified fluoride as the cause in 1931, and H. Trendley Dean established the dose-response relationship in the 1930s–1940s, **Grand Rapids, Michigan became the first city to fluoridate on January 25, 1945**. The 15-year trial monitoring nearly 30,000 children found a **greater than 60% reduction in dental caries**.

Fluoridation spread rapidly — endorsed by the WHO in 1969, named by the CDC in 1999 as one of the **"10 Great Public Health Achievements of the 20th Century."** Today, approximately 73% of the U.S. population on public water systems receives fluoridated water, though globally only about **5.4% of the world's population** does. Most of continental Europe has rejected fluoridation, with 11 European countries having stopped programs and 14 never having started, commonly citing ethics of mass medication and availability of fluoride toothpaste. Notably, European tooth decay rates have declined as steeply as American rates over the past 50 years despite the absence of water fluoridation.

A critical scientific evolution has been the recognition that fluoride's dental benefit is primarily **topical** (direct contact with tooth surfaces), not systemic (ingestion and incorporation into developing enamel) as originally believed. A 2011 European Commission review concluded that water fluoridation has no known advantage over topical prevention. This shift undermines a foundational rationale for water fluoridation: if the benefit is mainly topical, systemic ingestion — with its potential systemic risks — may be unnecessary.

The dental benefit itself has diminished over time. Early studies showed ~60% caries reduction; contemporary estimates range from **18–40%**, attributed to the "halo effect" of fluoride in processed foods and widespread fluoride toothpaste. The 2024 updated Cochrane Review found post-1975 studies showed fluoridation "may lead to slightly less tooth decay in children's baby teeth" but could **not confirm** it reduced decay in permanent teeth. The review noted that since 1975, increased fluoride toothpaste availability makes earlier results less applicable.

## 13. The 2024 court ruling and its cascading consequences

On September 24, 2024, Judge Edward Chen of the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California issued a ruling in *Food & Water Watch v. EPA* that sent shockwaves through public health. He found that **"fluoridation of water at 0.7 milligrams per liter poses an unreasonable risk of reduced IQ in children"** — the first successful citizen petition under TSCA Section 21 — and ordered EPA to initiate regulatory action. The ruling was explicit: "One thing the EPA cannot do, however, in the face of this Court's finding, is to ignore that risk."

The political and regulatory cascade since has been extraordinary. HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. announced plans to direct the CDC to stop recommending fluoridation and assembled an expert task force. **Utah became the first state to ban fluoridation** (signed March 27, 2025), followed by Florida (May 2025). Between September 2024 and April 2025, the Fluoride Action Network reported **62 communities** serving over 9 million people ended, suspended, or prevented fluoridation. The FDA announced action to remove ingestible fluoride prescription products for children, concluding by October 2025 that fluoride supplements "should not be used in children under age 3."

EPA filed an appeal to the Ninth Circuit in January 2025 and published a preliminary Fluoride Human Health Toxicity Assessment plan in January 2026. The appeal's opening brief focused on procedural and standing arguments rather than contesting the science directly. Oral arguments are expected in early 2026. Meanwhile, the ADA, AAP, and CDC continue to support community water fluoridation at 0.7 mg/L. The scientific landscape as of April 2026 is in unprecedented flux, with the regulatory outcome likely to reshape public health policy for decades.

## Conclusion: converging evidence, diverging policy

The century-long scientific investigation of fluoride reveals a compound whose risk profile is far more complex than either its champions or detractors have typically acknowledged. The evidence hierarchy is now reasonably clear: **skeletal fluorosis at high doses is unambiguous; dental fluorosis is widespread and increasing; neurodevelopmental effects on children's IQ above 1.5 mg/L are supported at moderate confidence by the largest systematic review ever conducted; thyroid disruption, pineal gland accumulation, and bone fracture risk at elevated exposures are biologically established.** Effects on creativity, abstract thinking, willpower, and personality remain essentially unstudied — a gap that, given the stakes, is itself a finding worth noting.

The central unresolved question — whether fluoride at the U.S. recommended level of 0.7 mg/L causes meaningful cognitive harm — cannot yet be definitively answered. The strongest prospective studies (Bashash, Green) suggest effects at exposure levels overlapping with those in fluoridated communities, but well-designed studies from New Zealand, Australia, and Sweden have found no such association. The discrepancy may reflect differences in total exposure measurement, confounding structures, or genuine population-level variation in vulnerability. What has changed irrevocably is the default assumption: the burden of proof is shifting. Where once fluoridation's safety was presumed and critics bore the burden of demonstrating harm, the NTP monograph, the federal court ruling, and the accumulating prospective cohort evidence have created a scientific environment where demonstrating adequate safety margins has become fluoridation proponents' central challenge. The next few years of regulatory action, appellate rulings, and new research will likely determine whether the 80-year experiment in community water fluoridation continues, contracts, or ends.
