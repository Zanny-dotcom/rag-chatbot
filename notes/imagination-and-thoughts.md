# How the brain builds thoughts, pictures, and the experience of being you

**The human brain generates thoughts through electrochemical cascades across ~86 billion neurons, constructs mental images by running its visual machinery in reverse, and assembles broad understanding by integrating memory, language, and prediction through a hierarchy stretching from raw sensation to the most abstract reaches of the default mode network.** No single "thought center" exists. Instead, cognition emerges from distributed cell assemblies wired by experience, orchestrated by prefrontal cortex, organized by neural oscillations, and broadcast into awareness through a global workspace. Yet the deepest puzzle remains: why any of this processing feels like something at all. This report traces the full arc — from synapse to self — combining accessible analogies with the technical neuroscience.

## Part I: How thoughts form — from electrical sparks to streams of consciousness

### The basic machinery: neurons, synapses, and the electrochemical substrate

Think of the brain as a city of **86 billion neurons**, each one a tiny decision-maker connected to thousands of neighbors by junctions called synapses. At rest, a neuron maintains a slight negative electrical charge (about **−70 millivolts**) across its membrane — like a coiled spring. When enough incoming signals push it past a threshold (~−50 mV), the spring releases: an **action potential** fires, a brief ~1-millisecond electrical spike that races down the neuron's long axon at speeds up to 120 meters per second.

At the synapse — a gap roughly 50 nanometers wide — this electrical signal converts to a chemical one. Calcium floods in, causing tiny vesicles to fuse with the membrane and release **neurotransmitter molecules** into the cleft. These molecules dock onto receptors on the receiving neuron. **Glutamate**, the brain's primary excitatory transmitter, pushes the next neuron toward firing. **GABA**, the primary inhibitor, holds it back. Modulatory transmitters — **dopamine** (encoding prediction errors and reward), **acetylcholine** (modulating attention and memory speed), **serotonin** and **norepinephrine** (tuning arousal and precision) — don't directly trigger firing but adjust the gain and sensitivity of entire circuits. The postsynaptic neuron integrates thousands of these excitatory and inhibitory inputs simultaneously, and if the sum crosses threshold, it fires in turn, propagating information forward.

The key to learning lies in **synaptic plasticity**. When a synapse is repeatedly active when the postsynaptic neuron fires, the connection strengthens — a process called **long-term potentiation (LTP)**, first demonstrated by Bliss and Lømo in 1973. The molecular gatekeeper is the **NMDA receptor**, which opens only when two conditions are met simultaneously: glutamate is bound and the membrane is already depolarized. This makes it a coincidence detector — the molecular implementation of associative learning. The reverse process, **long-term depression (LTD)**, weakens connections that are poorly correlated. Together, LTP and LTD sculpt the brain's wiring based on experience.

### Cell assemblies: how neurons become thoughts

In 1949, Donald Hebb proposed a deceptively simple idea that became the backbone of modern cognitive neuroscience. If neuron A repeatedly helps fire neuron B, the synapse between them strengthens. Carla Shatz later paraphrased this as **"neurons that fire together wire together."** From this principle, Hebb derived two larger concepts. First, **cell assemblies**: groups of neurons that become functionally linked through co-activation, forming the neural basis of a concept or idea. A cell assembly is not confined to one brain region — it can be a "diffuse structure comprising cells in the cortex and diencephalon" that briefly acts as a closed system. Second, **phase sequences**: chains of cell assemblies activated one after another, forming the neural substrate of a "train of thought."

Think of it like a choir. Individual singers (neurons) have limited power alone. But when a group rehearses together repeatedly, they synchronize into a cohesive ensemble (cell assembly) that can be activated as a unit. A medley of songs performed in sequence is a phase sequence — the biological basis of thinking one thing after another.

**Friedemann Pulvermüller** extended Hebb's framework to language, demonstrating that words are represented by distributed cell assemblies whose cortical geography reflects meaning. Action words like "lick," "pick," and "kick" activate motor cortex somatotopically — the tongue area for "lick," the hand area for "pick," the foot area for "kick" (Hauk, Johnsrude & Pulvermüller, 2004). In 2023, using brain-constrained deep neural networks, Pulvermüller showed that highly interconnected cell assemblies emerge as the biological basis of entire object categories, and that language accelerates concept formation — particularly for abstract concepts like "beauty" or "peace." Modern evidence includes **spike-timing-dependent plasticity** (Bi & Poo, 1998), which refined Hebb's rule: synapses strengthen when presynaptic spikes precede postsynaptic ones by milliseconds and weaken in reverse order. A 2022 finding by Lazari and colleagues demonstrated that Hebbian plasticity extends beyond synapses to **white matter and myelin** — co-stimulating cortical areas in a Hebbian fashion increased cortical excitability and myelin markers within the stimulated fiber bundle.

### The brain as a prediction machine: predictive processing

Perhaps the most powerful modern framework for understanding thought is **predictive processing**, which reframes the brain not as a passive receiver of information but as an active prediction engine. The core idea, rooted in Helmholtz's 1860 concept of "unconscious inference" and formalized by **Karl Friston** (UCL) as the **free energy principle**, is this: the brain constantly generates models of what it expects to encounter and compares those predictions against incoming sensory data. The difference — **prediction error** — is what drives learning and perception.

The analogy: imagine your brain as a newspaper editor who writes tomorrow's headline before the news happens. When events match the headline, the editor barely notices. When events diverge, the surprise signal (prediction error) propagates up through the hierarchy, demanding that the model be revised. Perception is not about building a picture from scratch each moment — it's about checking whether your existing picture is still accurate.

Neurally, this is implemented through a hierarchy of cortical areas. Each level sends **predictions downward** via feedback connections (from deep pyramidal neurons, mediated by slow NMDA receptors) and receives **prediction errors upward** via feedforward connections (from superficial pyramidal neurons, mediated by fast AMPA receptors). Friston and Kiebel (2009) laid out this architecture formally. Neuromodulators like dopamine and acetylcholine encode the **precision** — the confidence weighting — of prediction errors, adjusting how much the brain trusts its predictions versus incoming data.

**Andy Clark** (*Surfing Uncertainty*, 2015) synthesized this into a unified account of cognition: perception, action, and thought are all prediction error minimization. **Anil Seth** (University of Sussex, *Being You*, 2021) pushed it further, arguing that perception is a **"controlled hallucination"** — internally generated predictions constrained by sensory input. The brain doesn't receive reality; it constructs it from the inside out, checking its construction against the senses. Seth emphasizes that this applies to our sense of self too: the feeling of being "you" is an interoceptive prediction about your body's internal state.

### Global broadcast: how a thought becomes conscious

Not all neural processing reaches awareness. Most computation is local and modular — your visual cortex processes edges, your auditory cortex processes frequencies, your motor system plans movements, all without "you" necessarily knowing. So what makes a piece of information conscious?

**Bernard Baars** (1988) proposed **Global Workspace Theory (GWT)**: consciousness is what happens when information is **broadcast across a "global workspace"** and made simultaneously available to many specialized systems — memory, language, motor planning, decision-making. Think of a theater: unconscious processing is the bustling backstage work; consciousness is the bright spotlight on center stage, visible to the entire audience.

**Stanislas Dehaene** and Jean-Pierre Changeux gave this theory neural teeth with their **Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW)** model. The workspace is a distributed network of excitatory pyramidal neurons with **long-range axons** connecting prefrontal and parietal cortices. When a stimulus is strong enough or attended to, a dramatic event occurs: **"ignition."** An initial brief feedforward wave via fast AMPA connections is amplified by slower NMDA-mediated feedback connections, creating a self-sustained, reverberating state — a sudden, all-or-nothing cascade of activation across frontal and parietal regions. This ignition converts local, unconscious processing into globally broadcast conscious content. Missed stimuli evoke only transient, decaying activity; conscious stimuli show sustained late prefrontal activation and long-distance phase synchrony.

Gaillard et al. (2009) confirmed this in epilepsy patients with intracranial electrodes: conscious word perception activated frontal and parietal sites, while unconscious perception activated only occipital and temporal electrodes. The GNW predicts strict seriality — only one "ignited" representation at a time — which explains why consciousness feels like a single stream, even though unconscious processing is massively parallel.

### The brain talks to itself: Buzsáki's inside-out framework

Traditional neuroscience treats the brain as reactive: stimulus in, response out. **György Buzsáki** (NYU), in *The Brain from Inside Out* (2019), argues this "outside-in" view has become a straitjacket. Instead, the brain is fundamentally a **self-organized system** with preconfigured internal dynamics. Before any experience, the brain generates patterns of activity — "nonsense sequences" — that are initially meaningless but become grounded through the consequences of action. Cognition, in this view, is **internalized action**: once circuits are calibrated by moving through the world, the brain can disengage from sensors and run "what if" simulations internally.

The evidence is striking. **Preplay** (Dragoi & Tonegawa, 2011, *Nature*) showed that hippocampal place-cell sequences occur during rest *before* a novel spatial experience — the brain pre-organizes cell assemblies for future use. **Pastalkova et al. (2008, *Science*)** demonstrated that sequential firing patterns in the hippocampus during a memory task were internally generated, not stimulus-driven, and predicted the animal's upcoming choice with ~90% accuracy. Buzsáki's framework reframes thought not as a response to the world but as **an exploration of the brain's own internally generated dynamics**.

### Neural oscillations: the brain's temporal scaffolding

Brain rhythms — oscillations at different frequencies — provide the temporal framework that organizes cell assemblies into coherent sequences.

**Gamma oscillations (30–100+ Hz)** serve local processing and feature binding. Pascal Fries' **"Communication through Coherence"** hypothesis (2005, 2015) proposes that neurons communicate effectively when synchronized at gamma frequency, creating temporal windows for information exchange. Wolf Singer demonstrated that gamma synchronization in visual cortex reflects global stimulus properties — potentially solving the **binding problem** of how distributed feature representations unite into a single percept.

**Theta oscillations (4–8 Hz)** organize memory and sequential processing. The theta-gamma code, proposed by **John Lisman and Ole Jensen (2013, *Neuron*)**, is elegantly simple: different items are represented in different gamma subcycles nested within a single theta cycle. Roughly **7±2 gamma cycles** fit within one theta cycle — remarkably corresponding to the classic capacity limit of working memory. Buzsáki calls these nested sequences **"neural words"** — the brain's syntax. Tort et al. (2009, *PNAS*) showed that theta-gamma coupling in the hippocampus increased during learning and correlated directly with performance accuracy.

**Alpha oscillations (8–12 Hz)** gate information flow, regulating which signals enter conscious processing through inhibitory suppression. During sleep, **slow oscillations (~0.5–1 Hz)**, sleep spindles, and hippocampal sharp-wave ripples coordinate to transfer memories from hippocampus to neocortex — the brain's overnight filing system.

### Building new thoughts: the compositional architecture

How does the brain think a thought it has never thought before? You can understand "the dog chased the man" and immediately understand "the man chased the dog" — same words, different meaning. **Steven Frankland and Joshua Greene** (Harvard/Princeton, 2015, *PNAS*) discovered the neural architecture for this compositional thought. Using fMRI, they found two adjacent regions in the **left mid-superior temporal cortex (lmSTC)** that encode different thematic roles: a more medial region for the **agent** ("who did it") and a more lateral region for the **patient** ("to whom it was done"). Crucially, the brain **reuses the same activity patterns across different sentences** — patterns function like variables or placeholders that can be filled with any concept. As Greene put it: "You can't have neurons just waiting for someone to say 'Joe Biden beat Vladimir Putin at Scrabble.' There has to be a system for forming meanings on the fly."

In 2020, Frankland and Greene expanded this, identifying the **anterior-medial prefrontal cortex** as sharing noun-verb conjunction representations and the hippocampus as flexibly binding event elements. Their comprehensive review proposed two complementary systems: **map-like representations** in the default mode network and **sentence-like representations** of conceptual relations in temporal and prefrontal cortex. The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex furnishes instructions for possible thoughts; the DMN serves as the canvas on which thoughts are drawn.

### The prefrontal conductor: orchestrating the stream of thought

**Avgusta Shestyuk and Robert Knight** (UC Berkeley, 2018, *Nature Human Behaviour*) performed landmark work tracking thoughts through the brain in real time. Using **electrocorticography (ECoG)** — hundreds of electrodes placed directly on the brain surface of 16 epilepsy patients — they tracked neural activity across eight cognitive tasks ranging from simple word repetition to complex antonym generation.

They found a **universal four-phase sequence**: sensory detection (visual/auditory cortex activates), meaning extraction (sensory + prefrontal cortex jointly extract meaning), PFC coordination (prefrontal cortex recruits additional networks for difficult tasks over 2–3 seconds), and response generation (PFC "stands down" as motor cortex activates). The prefrontal cortex was **continuously active throughout**, coordinating input from different brain areas like a conductor leading an orchestra. Knight called it **"the glue of cognition."** One surprising finding: the brain prepares motor areas very early — before knowing what the response will be — which may explain why people sometimes speak before they think. Increased difficulty doesn't make neurons work harder; instead, **more cortical areas get recruited** by the PFC.

## Part II: The mind's eye — how the brain paints pictures with eyes closed

### Running the visual system in reverse

When you close your eyes and picture a red apple, something remarkable happens: your brain's visual cortex — the same tissue that processes light from your retinas — **activates from the top down, in the reverse direction of normal seeing.** Instead of information flowing from eyes → V1 → V2 → higher regions, imagery runs the circuit backward: memory and semantic regions → higher visual areas → early visual cortex. You are, in a sense, hallucinating on purpose.

**Stephen Kosslyn** (Harvard) provided the foundational evidence. His PET studies in the 1990s showed that mental imagery activates topographically organized visual cortex, including **primary visual cortex (V1/Area 17)**. When participants visualized letters at different sizes, V1 activation shifted exactly as it would for seen images — small mental images activated foveal (central) regions, large ones activated peripheral representations (Kosslyn, Thompson, Kim & Alpert, 1995). Slotnick, Thompson, and Kosslyn (2005, *Cerebral Cortex*) demonstrated that mental imagery evokes cortical activity with **precise retinotopic organization** — imagined objects map onto the visual cortex in the same spatial arrangement as perceived objects, strongly supporting the "depictive" view that mental images are picture-like rather than purely symbolic.

The reversal of information flow was directly demonstrated by **Dentico et al. (2014, *NeuroImage*)** using high-density EEG: both Granger causality and dynamic causal modeling revealed **increased top-down signal flow** in parieto-occipital cortex during imagery compared to perception. Dijkstra, Zeidman, and colleagues (2017, *Scientific Reports*) showed via fMRI that experienced vividness during imagery was selectively associated with increases in top-down connectivity to early visual cortex. Mechelli et al. (2004, *Cerebral Cortex*) pinpointed the source: during perception, bottom-up connections from inferior occipital cortex drove category-specific ventral temporal activation, while during imagery, **top-down connections from prefrontal cortex** drove the same regions.

### The Fusiform Imagery Node: a newly discovered hub for mental pictures

A paradigm-shifting model emerged in 2021 when **Spagna, Hajhajate, Liu, and Bartolomeo** published a meta-analysis of 46 fMRI studies in *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*. They found that visual mental imagery reliably engages a **well-delimited region in the left fusiform gyrus** — which they named the **Fusiform Imagery Node (FIN)** — and a Bayesian analysis showed **no reliable evidence for imagery-related activity in early visual cortices** across studies. This was a direct challenge to the Kosslyn tradition.

The FIN is domain-general: it supports imagery for objects, colors, words, faces, and spatial relationships. Ultra-high-field **7T fMRI** studies (Hajhajate et al., 2023/2025) showed it encodes semantic information, sharing multivoxel patterns with left inferior frontal gyrus and intraparietal sulcus. Critically, in people with **aphantasia**, similar visual areas activated during imagery attempts, but the **FIN was functionally disconnected from fronto-parietal areas** — suggesting that the subjective absence of imagery reflects broken connectivity, not absent visual processing. Lesion network mapping (Kutsche et al., 2026, *Cortex*) confirmed this: all lesions causing acquired loss of mental imagery were functionally connected to the left FIN — **100% sensitivity** — providing causal evidence for its role.

The emerging picture is that fronto-parietal networks initiate and maintain imagery activity in a core temporal network centered on the FIN, which bridges semantic knowledge and visual representation. This sits alongside, rather than replacing, the evidence for V1 involvement in high-resolution imagery tasks.

### Different layers for seeing versus imagining

One of the most elegant recent discoveries comes from **laminar fMRI** at submillimeter resolution using 7T scanners. The cortex has six layers, and feedforward (perceptual) signals arrive primarily in **middle layers** (layer 4), while feedback signals target **deep and superficial layers**. Lawrence, Formisano, and Muckli (2024, *Nature Communications*) showed that in V1, mental imagery content was decodable from **deep layers only**, while illusory perceptual content was decodable from **superficial layers only**. Iamshchinina et al. (2024, *iScience*) found a complementary pattern in high-level visual cortex: during perception, activity was uniform or increased toward the surface; during imagery, it **decreased from deep through middle to superficial** — consistent with internally generated feedback targeting deep layers.

This laminar segregation may be how the brain simultaneously processes perception and imagery without confusing them: feedforward signals and feedback signals operate in different cortical compartments, like two-way traffic on separate lanes.

### How the brain tells imagination from reality

If imagery and perception share the same neural hardware, how does the brain avoid confusing them? **Nadine Dijkstra** (UCL) and colleagues answered this in a 2025 *Neuron* paper: the **fusiform gyrus** generates a "reality signal" whose activation level predicts whether a person judges an experience as real or imagined — regardless of what it actually is. Usually imagery generates weaker fusiform activity than perception, keeping them safely apart. But when imagery is very vivid, fusiform activation can cross a **"reality threshold,"** causing confusion. The **anterior insula** and **dorsomedial prefrontal cortex** then evaluate this signal and render a binary judgment: real or imagined.

This threshold mechanism has clinical implications. In **schizophrenia**, an altered reality threshold may allow internally generated imagery to cross into "real" territory, producing hallucinations. In a study of over 600 participants (Dijkstra & Fleming, 2023, *Nature Communications*), people with higher overall imagery vividness made **more reality-monitoring errors** — both false alarms and misses — because imagined and perceived signals are intermixed in the brain, and reality judgments depend on their combined sensory strength. This echoes the **Perky Effect** (1910), in which participants failed to distinguish faintly projected images from their own mental imagery.

### Aphantasia and hyperphantasia: the extremes of the mind's eye

About **2–5% of people** cannot voluntarily visualize at all. **Adam Zeman** (University of Exeter) coined the term **aphantasia** in 2015, based on patient MX — a retired surveyor who lost imagery ability after cardiac surgery. fMRI showed MX's visual cortex activated only for viewed images, not imagined ones; he instead recruited frontal networks. The term itself derives from Aristotle's *phantasia* (the mind's eye), with the privative prefix "a-."

A critical finding emerged in 2025: **Chang et al. (*Current Biology*)** showed that when people with aphantasia attempt to visualize, their **V1 still activates with content-specific patterns** — algorithms can decode what they were trying to imagine. Yet they report no conscious visual imagery whatsoever. Joel Pearson (UNSW) described it as "the brain doing the math but skipping the final step of showing the result on a screen." However, Scholz, Monzel, and Liu (2025, *Current Biology*) noted these V1 patterns are fundamentally different from perception — ipsilateral rather than contralateral, and not cross-decodable with perceptual representations — meaning they are not simply "unconscious images" but qualitatively different neural states. Interestingly, **~60% of aphantasics dream visually**, suggesting the brain retains the capacity for imagery but cannot deploy it voluntarily. Aphantasia is associated with reduced autobiographical memory, face recognition difficulties, and elevated autistic spectrum traits, and runs in families more often than chance.

At the other extreme, **hyperphantasia** (~2.5–3% of the population) involves imagery so vivid it rivals actual perception. Zeman et al. (2021, *Cerebral Cortex Communications*) found **stronger resting-state connectivity between prefrontal cortex and visual networks** in hyperphantasics. Keogh, Bergmann, and Pearson (2020, *eLife*) provided causal evidence: lower resting excitability in V1 predicts stronger imagery, and using **tDCS to decrease V1 excitability actually increased imagery strength** — counterintuitively, quieting the visual cortex lets top-down signals dominate. The subjective vividness spectrum is captured by the **Vividness of Visual Imagery Questionnaire (VVIQ)**, developed by David Marks in 1973, now used in over 2,000 studies with excellent reliability.

### Beyond pictures: imagery across all the senses

Mental imagery is not exclusively visual. **Motor imagery** activates the supplementary motor area, premotor cortex, and even primary motor cortex — overlapping extensively with actual movement execution (Hétu et al., 2013 meta-analysis). Imagined movements preserve the temporal structure of real movements (Jeannerod's Motor Simulation Theory), which is why motor imagery is used in **stroke rehabilitation** and elite athletic training. **Auditory imagery** — hearing a song in your head — activates secondary auditory cortex and supplementary motor areas (Zatorre & Halpern, 1996). **Olfactory imagery** is the weakest modality for most people but still engages piriform cortex and orbitofrontal cortex, with sniffing patterns during imagined smells resembling those during real ones (Bensafi et al., 2003, *Nature Neuroscience*). A 2025 *Neuron* study found that mental imagery and perception overlap predominantly within **transmodal association networks** (the default mode network) rather than adjacent unimodal sensory cortex — suggesting imagination may fundamentally be a property of association cortex.

## Part III: Seeing the forest — how the brain assembles context and the big picture

### The default mode network: the brain's storyteller

When you stop focusing on the outside world — when you daydream, remember the past, imagine the future, or consider another person's perspective — a specific set of brain regions activates in concert. **Marcus Raichle** discovered this pattern in 2001, naming it the **default mode network (DMN)** after observing that certain areas consistently *decreased* activity during demanding tasks compared to quiet rest. As of 2023, over **8,000 studies** have investigated the DMN (Menon, 2023, *Neuron*).

Think of the DMN as the brain's storytelling department. Its core regions — **medial prefrontal cortex** (self-processing, autobiographical memory, future goals), **posterior cingulate cortex/precuneus** (a central connectivity hub), **angular gyrus** (linking perception, memory, and spatial cognition), and **hippocampal formation** (memory construction) — form a tightly interconnected system that constructs an ongoing internal narrative. Andrews-Hanna (2012) identified two subsystems: a **medial temporal lobe subsystem** (hippocampus, parahippocampal cortex, retrosplenial cortex) supporting episodic/scene construction, and a **dorsal medial subsystem** (dorsomedial PFC, temporoparietal junction, temporal pole) supporting social cognition and theory of mind.

Menon's 2023 unifying model proposes that the DMN integrates episodic memory, language, and semantic representations to create a **coherent "internal narrative"** central to self-construction and consciousness. The DMN sustains "frames of thought," and its hub properties facilitate network switching and global state changes; mind wandering is a natural outcome of this dynamical process. During narrative comprehension, DMN correlation patterns dynamically reconfigure to track meaning across extended temporal scales — coupling strength between DMN nodes predicts how well people remember story segments (Simony et al., 2016, *Nature Communications*).

### Working memory: the brain's mental workbench

To think about anything complex, you need to hold multiple pieces of information active simultaneously. This is **working memory** — the brain's temporary workspace. **Alan Baddeley** and Graham Hitch (1974) proposed the classic multicomponent model: a **phonological loop** (holding speech-based information via rehearsal, with ~2-second capacity), a **visuospatial sketchpad** (manipulating visual and spatial information), and a **central executive** (a supervisory controller in the frontal lobes that directs focus, suppresses irrelevance, and coordinates subsystems). In 2000, Baddeley added the **episodic buffer** — a limited-capacity store that binds information from all subsystems and long-term memory into unified episodes, functioning as a "modeling space" for mental simulation.

Neurally, **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC)** is central to working memory's executive control. Joaquin Fuster first reported persistent neural activity in macaque PFC during delay periods in 1971, and Patricia Goldman-Rakic (1995) established that individual neurons maintain stimulus-selective firing that bridges the gap between encoding and response. However, more recent work (Curtis & D'Esposito, 2014, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*) has revised the canonical picture: sensory cortex, not PFC alone, maintains high-fidelity representations of working memory content. The PFC instead provides top-down **bias signals** that keep relevant representations active in sensory regions — like a librarian who doesn't store all the books but knows exactly which shelves to keep illuminated.

### The binding problem: unifying a fragmented world

Your experience right now feels unified — a single scene with shapes, colors, sounds, and emotions blended seamlessly. Yet the brain processes these features in separate, specialized areas: color in V4, motion in V5/MT, faces in the fusiform face area, sounds in auditory cortex. The **binding problem** asks: how are these distributed representations combined into a single coherent experience?

**Christoph von der Malsburg** (1981) proposed the most influential answer: **binding by synchrony**. Neurons coding features of the same object synchronize their firing at gamma frequencies (30–100 Hz), while neurons coding different objects fire out of sync. Wolf Singer and colleagues (1989, *Nature*) demonstrated gamma-band synchronization in cat visual cortex that reflected global stimulus properties. Pascal Fries extended this into the **Communication through Coherence** framework: phase synchronization at gamma frequencies permits separate channels of communication between brain regions.

However, the binding-by-synchrony hypothesis faces challenges. **Roelfsema (2023, *Neuron*)** argued that enhanced **firing rates**, not oscillatory synchrony, bind features into coherent object representations. Thiele and Stoner found that perceptual binding of moving patterns had no effect on synchronization. The current consensus is that multiple mechanisms likely contribute: rate coding, temporal synchrony, re-entrant signaling (Lamme), and the laminar segregation of feedforward and feedback processing described earlier.

### The hippocampus: memory's construction site

The hippocampus is not a filing cabinet that stores complete memories. It is more like an **index system** that stores pointers to distributed cortical representations. When you recall an event, the hippocampus performs **pattern completion** — retrieving a full memory from a partial cue by reactivating the cortical patterns present at encoding. David Marr laid the computational groundwork in 1971, and Teyler and DiScenna (1986) formalized it as **hippocampal indexing theory**.

Horner et al. (2015, *Nature Communications*) provided elegant evidence: participants learned complex multimodal events, and at retrieval, neocortical activity corresponding to *all* event elements was reinstated — including ones incidental to the task. The degree of this reinstatement correlated with hippocampal activity. Grande et al. (2019, *Journal of Neuroscience*) used 7T fMRI to show that the **CA3 subfield** specifically drives this holistic recollection. Memory is also constructive — **Barron et al. (2023, *Nature Human Behaviour*)** demonstrated computationally how hippocampal replay trains generative models to recreate sensory experiences from latent variable representations, explaining not just recall but imagination, future thinking, and even systematic memory distortions like boundary extension.

### Semantic hubs and the architecture of meaning

How does the brain know that an apple is a fruit, red, sweet, and round — all at once? The **hub-and-spoke model** (Patterson, Lambon Ralph, Rogers) proposes that modality-specific "spokes" — visual features in fusiform cortex, action knowledge in motor regions, auditory features in superior temporal areas — converge on a transmodal "hub" in the **anterior temporal lobe (ATL)**, specifically ventrolateral ATL. This hub integrates information from all spokes into coherent, pan-modal concepts.

The most compelling evidence comes from **semantic dementia**: patients with focal ATL atrophy show progressive, cross-modal conceptual breakdown — rejecting "emu" as a bird while still accepting "butterfly." Pobric et al. (2010) induced similar impairments in healthy participants by disrupting ATL with repetitive TMS, confirming its causal role. Diffusion-tensor imaging reveals that white-matter tracts from multiple modality-specific regions converge physically at the ATL (Binney et al., 2012), providing the structural conduits for integration. Chiou et al. (2019, *NeuroImage*) showed that the ATL dynamically forms flexible long-range networks with domain-specific spokes, acting as a semantic "pivot" with directional influence flowing predominantly hub-to-spoke.

### The principal gradient: from sensation to abstraction

The cortex is organized along a single dominant axis. **Margulies et al. (2016, *PNAS*)**, using connectivity data from 820 adults in the Human Connectome Project, identified the **principal gradient** of cortical organization: one end anchored in primary sensory/motor regions, the other in the transmodal default mode network. DMN regions sit at the greatest geodesic distance from all sensory landmarks — they are, anatomically and functionally, **maximally abstracted from the sensory world**.

This gradient captures the order of canonical brain networks: sensory-motor → dorsal attention → ventral attention → frontoparietal control → DMN. It builds on Mesulam's (1998) hierarchy from unimodal to transmodal cortex and has profound functional implications: sensory areas represent short events in high-dimensional detail, while transmodal DMN regions represent longer narrative arcs in lower-dimensional, generalized form. Gao et al. (2022, *eLife*) showed the gradient "flexes" with task demands — strong semantic associations strengthen the gradient (separating sensory from abstract processing), while weak associations requiring controlled retrieval compress it. This is the brain's architecture for moving from raw sensation to meaning.

### Scene construction: how the brain builds mental worlds

Remembering the past, imagining the future, and constructing hypothetical scenarios all share a common process: **scene construction** — the assembly of disparate multimodal elements into a coherent spatial context. **Demis Hassabis and Eleanor Maguire** (2007, 2009) proposed that this is the hippocampus's primary contribution to cognition, not memory storage per se but the ability to **bind elements into spatially coherent wholes**.

Four of five patients with hippocampal amnesia studied by Hassabis et al. (2007, *PNAS*) could not construct imaginary scenarios — their descriptions were fragmented, lacked spatial coherence, and were dramatically impoverished. fMRI revealed a distributed network for scene construction: bilateral hippocampus, parahippocampal gyrus, retrosplenial cortex, posterior parietal cortex, medial PFC — overlapping heavily with the DMN. This network activated whether participants recalled real memories, recalled previously imagined experiences, or constructed entirely novel imaginary scenarios. The complementary **Constructive Episodic Simulation Hypothesis** (Schacter & Addis, 2007) emphasizes that episodic memory's flexibility enables recombination of past elements for future simulation — the hippocampus doesn't just replay memories; it **remixes** them.

### Mind-wandering: the spontaneous generation of thought

Your mind wanders roughly **30–50% of your waking life**. Far from being a failure of attention, spontaneous thought is a fundamental mode of cognition. **Kalina Christoff** and colleagues (2016, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*) proposed a dynamic framework: mind-wandering belongs to a family of spontaneous-thought phenomena — including creative thinking and dreaming — that vary along dimensions of **deliberate constraints** (cognitive control from frontoparietal networks) and **automatic constraints** (sensory/affective salience from the salience network). Dreaming sits at one extreme (minimal constraints), goal-directed thought at the other, with mind-wandering and creativity in between.

Critically, Christoff et al. (2009) showed that mind-wandering involves **concurrent activation of the default network AND executive networks** — challenging the simple "DMN on, executive off" dichotomy. Ellamil et al. (2016) used neurophenomenology with trained meditators to show that the **medial temporal lobe subsystem peaks in activation *before* a spontaneous thought begins**, while the frontoparietal control network peaks *after* — suggesting the hippocampal system initiates spontaneous thoughts while executive circuits evaluate and monitor them. Mind-wandering depends on three processes: perceptual decoupling (disengaging from the external world), mental improvisation (generating novel content), and mental navigation (projecting across time, space, and other minds).

## Part IV: Why any of this feels like something — the consciousness puzzle

### The hard problem: the deepest question in science

Everything described so far — cell assemblies, prediction errors, global broadcast, scene construction — could in principle happen "in the dark," without any inner experience. A robot could process predictions, broadcast information globally, and construct scenes without anything *feeling like* anything. So why does it feel like something to be you?

**David Chalmers** (1995) crystallized this as the **"hard problem" of consciousness**. He distinguished "easy" problems — explaining cognitive functions like discrimination, attention, behavioral control, all solvable in principle by identifying mechanisms — from the hard problem: why is there "something it is like" (Nagel, 1974) to undergo these processes? Even complete knowledge of every neuron's firing pattern would leave unanswered *why* those patterns produce the felt quality of redness, the ache of pain, or the sound of rain. Joseph Levine (1983) called this the **"explanatory gap"** — physical explanations specify mechanisms and functions, but subjective experience seems to be something above and beyond functional roles.

Chalmers' **zombie argument** illustrates the problem: we can coherently conceive of a being physically identical to a human but utterly lacking consciousness — a "philosophical zombie." If such a being is conceivable, then physical facts alone don't logically necessitate consciousness. Frank Jackson's **Mary's Room** thought experiment (1982) makes a similar point: a scientist who knows everything physical about color vision still learns something new upon first seeing red. Responses range from **Type-A materialism** (Daniel Dennett: the hard problem rests on confused intuitions) to **nonphysicalism** (Chalmers himself: consciousness may be a fundamental feature of nature requiring new psychophysical laws).

### Neural correlates of consciousness: mapping where experience lives

Setting aside the hard problem, a pragmatic research program asks: which brain processes reliably correlate with conscious experience? **Francis Crick and Christof Koch** (1990) launched this program, defining **neural correlates of consciousness (NCCs)** as "the minimal neuronal mechanisms jointly sufficient for any one specific conscious percept."

Key experimental paradigms have been revelatory. In **binocular rivalry**, different images shown to each eye compete for awareness. Leopold and Logothetis (1996) showed that neurons in early visual areas (V1) correlated more with the physical stimulus, while neurons in **inferior temporal cortex tracked the conscious percept** — what the subject actually saw. In **visual masking**, Dehaene et al. (2001) showed visible words activated widespread fronto-parietal networks while masked (invisible) words activated only early sensory areas. **Dreaming paradigms** (Siclari et al., 2017) contrasted brain activity during dreaming versus dreamless sleep within NREM, finding that conscious dreams correlated with reduced low-frequency activity in a **posterior parieto-occipital "hot zone."** The **Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI)** (Casali et al., 2013, *Science Translational Medicine*) uses TMS-EEG to estimate consciousness levels: awake volunteers show PCI of **0.31–0.70**; values below 0.31 indicate unconsciousness.

Koch et al. (2016, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*) distinguished two dimensions: the **level** of consciousness (arousal — awake vs. coma) and the **content** of consciousness (what is experienced). Crucially, discovering NCCs identifies *where* and *when* consciousness occurs but does not explain *why* those processes feel like something.

### Four theories compete to explain consciousness

**Global Workspace Theory**, covered above, explains consciousness as global broadcast: information becomes conscious when it is amplified and made available across the brain through prefrontal-parietal ignition. Its strength is clear functional utility — consciousness enables flexible, novel behavior. Its weakness is that it may conflate consciousness with *cognitive access*: perhaps information can be conscious without being globally broadcast.

**Integrated Information Theory (IIT)**, developed by **Giulio Tononi** (first proposed 2004; IIT 4.0 published 2023 in *PLoS Computational Biology*), takes a radically different approach. It starts from five axioms of experience — intrinsic existence, composition, information, integration, exclusion — and derives postulates that a physical substrate must satisfy. The central quantity is **Φ (phi)** — integrated information measuring how much a system's causal structure is irreducible to independent parts. IIT claims consciousness *is* integrated information: a system is conscious if and only if Φ > 0, and the quality of experience corresponds to the system's cause-effect structure. IIT predicts that the **posterior cortex** (parietal, temporal, occipital) is the primary substrate of consciousness due to its grid-like connectivity architecture maximizing Φ, while the prefrontal cortex's more modular architecture generates less integrated information. IIT has panpsychist implications — any system with Φ > 0 possesses some consciousness — and predicts that standard digital computers, being feedforward, have Φ = 0 and are therefore unconscious regardless of computational power.

**Higher-Order Theories (HOT)**, developed by **David Rosenthal** and extended by **Hakwan Lau** and **Joseph LeDoux** (Brown, Lau & LeDoux, 2019, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*), propose that a mental state becomes conscious when it is the target of a **higher-order representation** — a thought about that thought. First-order representations in sensory cortex encode stimuli; higher-order representations in **prefrontal cortex** (especially dlPFC) re-represent those states. Consciousness arises from this meta-cognitive layer. The theory explains blindsight (V1 damage eliminates conscious vision but not discrimination, consistent with preserved first-order representations lacking higher-order re-representation) and emotional consciousness (feelings arise only when higher-order circuits re-represent subcortical states, per LeDoux & Brown, 2017, *PNAS*).

**Recurrent Processing Theory (RPT)**, developed by **Victor Lamme** (2006), proposes that consciousness arises from recurrent (feedback) processing in sensory cortex, not requiring frontal involvement — aligned with the posterior hot zone view. Local recurrent loops within visual cortex may be sufficient for basic visual experience.

### The front-versus-back debate and the adversarial collaboration

The central anatomical question in consciousness science: is consciousness primarily in the **posterior "hot zone"** (temporal, parietal, occipital cortex) or in **prefrontal regions**? Koch, Tononi, and colleagues (Boly et al., 2017, *Journal of Neuroscience*) marshaled evidence for the posterior view: bilateral frontal lobectomy does not abolish consciousness; dream contents can be decoded from posterior but not anterior cortex; selective posterior lesions abolish specific modalities of experience (V4 lesions eliminate color experience). The frontal camp (Dehaene, Lau, Odegaard et al., 2017) countered that posterior activity may be necessary but not sufficient, and that no-report paradigms may underestimate frontal contributions.

The **COGITATE adversarial collaboration** — published in **Nature in 2025** (Ferrante, Cogitate Consortium, Melloni et al., DOI: 10.1038/s41586-025-08888-1) — tested preregistered predictions of both IIT and GNW using 256 participants recorded with fMRI, MEG, and intracranial EEG. The results challenged both theories. Conscious content information appeared in visual, ventrotemporal, *and* inferior frontal cortex. Sustained responses in occipital and temporal cortex reflected stimulus duration, **partially supporting IIT's posterior cortex predictions**. Content-specific synchronization between frontal and early visual areas **supported GNW on connectivity**. But IIT's predicted sustained synchronization *within* posterior cortex was not found, and GNW's predicted ignition at stimulus offset was absent with limited content representation in PFC. **Christof Koch conceded a famous 25-year bet with David Chalmers** (paying with a case of fine wine) because NCCs had not been fully identified by 2023.

### Why neural activity alone does not equal experience

The aphantasia findings crystallize a central mystery. When people with aphantasia try to visualize, their **V1 activates with content-specific patterns** decodable by algorithms — yet they experience nothing visual. The brain is processing visual information but "skipping the final step of showing the result on a screen." This demonstrates that V1 activation alone is insufficient for conscious imagery. A theory paper proposes that conscious imagery requires not just activation but **integration** (binding visual features) and **amplification** (enhancement for conscious access) supported by a fronto-parietal-fusiform network. Aphantasia reflects deficits in top-down modulation — preserved generation but impaired integration and amplification.

This pattern repeats across neuroscience. Subliminal stimuli activate early sensory areas and influence behavior without conscious experience (Dehaene et al., 2006). Blindsight patients discriminate visual stimuli without seeing. During anesthesia, ~20% of patients retain some neural processing without awareness. Prosopagnosia patients show differential physiological responses to familiar faces despite no conscious recognition. In every case, extensive neural processing occurs without consciousness. **Neural activity is necessary but not sufficient** — something additional must transform processing into felt experience.

### Where the field stands in 2026

As of early 2026, the consciousness field is characterized by productive tension. The COGITATE results challenged both IIT and GNWT substantially; neither was vindicated. A September 2023 open letter signed by prominent neuroscientists characterized IIT as "pseudoscience" (reiterated in Baxter et al., 2025, *Nature Neuroscience*), though a field survey found only a minority fully endorsed this label. Anil Seth and Tim Bayne (2022, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*) provided a systematic framework for comparing theories, and new approaches are emerging: the **Predictive Global Neuronal Workspace** model (Whyte & Smith, 2021) unifies GNW with Bayesian active inference; data-driven methods (Overgaard et al., 2025) supplement theory-driven approaches; and a **Multiple Generator Hypothesis** (2025) suggests consciousness may arise from multiple mechanisms rather than one. The hard problem remains unsolved. Even perfect NCC identification would leave unexplained *why* those processes feel like something. But the field's methodological sophistication — adversarial collaborations, no-report paradigms, multimodal neuroimaging, laminar fMRI — is raising the bar for every theory.

## Conclusion: what we know and what remains mysterious

The mechanistic picture is increasingly clear. Thoughts form through **Hebbian cell assemblies** wired by experience and organized temporally by **theta-gamma oscillatory scaffolding**. The brain operates as a **prediction engine** that generates hierarchical models of the world and updates them through prediction error. New thoughts are composed from conceptual building blocks using **variable-like representations** in temporal and prefrontal cortex. The prefrontal cortex orchestrates this process, linking perception to action as the "glue of cognition." When information crosses an ignition threshold, it is **broadcast globally** through prefrontal-parietal workspace networks, becoming consciously accessible.

Mental imagery runs this machinery in reverse, with top-down signals from memory and semantic regions reactivating visual cortex — in different cortical layers than perception uses, enabling the brain to keep imagination and reality separate through laminar segregation and a fusiform "reality threshold." The **Fusiform Imagery Node** emerges as a critical hub, and its disconnection from fronto-parietal networks explains aphantasia. Broad-picture understanding is assembled along a **principal gradient** from sensory to abstract processing, with the default mode network at the abstract apex integrating episodic memory, semantic knowledge, and language into an ongoing internal narrative. The hippocampus constructs scenes by binding elements into spatial contexts, and spontaneous thought arises from the interplay of default, executive, and salience networks.

The deepest insight from this research may be the most unsettling: **the brain generates experience, not merely processes information, and we do not yet understand how.** Aphantasia reveals that neural activity can exist without experience. Subliminal processing shows information can influence behavior without awareness. No current theory — not Global Workspace, not Integrated Information, not Higher-Order Theories — fully explains why any of this processing feels like something. The hard problem persists not because we lack data but because we may lack the right conceptual framework. What has changed is that the question is now empirical, not merely philosophical. Adversarial collaborations, laminar imaging, and increasingly precise experimental paradigms are forcing theories to make falsifiable predictions and face the evidence. The brain's ability to think about its own thinking may yet be the key that unlocks the mystery — or it may be the deepest illustration of its limits.
